# The Crow's Nest Vantage and the Maguire 1876 Map: A Coordinate-Provenance Dossier for Terrain-Accurate Little Bighorn Simulation

## TL;DR
- The single strongest candidate for the true Crow's Nest vantage is the OpenStreetMap-tagged peak "Traditional Crows Nest?" at **45.4454, -107.1392** (elevation 4,439 ft / 1,353 m), on the Wolf Mountains crest ~21 km ESE of Last Stand Hill, on private Mountain Pocket family land — but its provenance is crowd-sourced and unverified, so it must be treated as MEDIUM-LOW confidence pending field GPS or specialist confirmation.
- There is **no GNIS record** for the historic Crow's Nest vantage; the only GNIS "Crows Nest Camp" (FID 770355, at 45.5855195, -107.8406625) is an unrelated locale ~30 km WEST of the battlefield and a false hit, while the National Park Service interpretive markers near Reno-Benteen (HMDB coordinates 45.52203, -107.37835 and 45.52595, -107.38372) are the confirmed **decoys** — roadside signs, not the vantage.
- Accessible Maguire 1876 map scans exist: a high-resolution IIIF image at Northwestern University Libraries and the full public-domain *Annual Report of the Chief of Engineers* (1876) on Internet Archive; at least two Maguire variants exist (the 1876 field-survey "first map" and the reworked printed "Version 8").

## Key Findings

### The decoy is confirmed and must be excluded
The task's warning is correct. The NPS/HMDB "Crow's Nest" marker sits at **45° 31.322′ N, 107° 22.701′ W** (= 45.52203, -107.37835). Its verbatim text reads: "Crow's Nest. June 25, 1876. From this promontory 13 miles away in the Wolf Mountains, Custer's scouts observe the Lakota, and Cheyenne pony herd and evidence of a large village in the valley behind you," and it is located "in Crow Agency, Montana, in Big Horn County … on U.S. 212 one mile east of Interstate 90 … near the end of the battlefield drive." A companion "Custer's Advance" marker is at 45° 31.557′ N, 107° 23.023′ W (= 45.52595, -107.38372), "on Little Bighorn Battlefield Road … near the Reno-Benteen Battlefield." Both are inside/adjacent to the national monument, roughly 20 km from the real vantage. They are interpretive signage placed for visitor access and should be scored as decoys, not candidates.

### GNIS returns no true-vantage record
A search of the Geographic Names Information System surfaces only "Crows Nest Camp," FID 770355, a cultural "locale" that per the GNIS record (via TopoZone/HometownLocator) is "displayed on the 'Woody Creek Camp' USGS topo map. The latitude and longitude coordinates … are 45.5855195 (North), -107.8406625 (West) and the approximate elevation is 3,530 feet (1,076 meters)." This lies near St. Xavier, roughly 30 km WEST of the battlefield, and is unrelated to the 1876 observation point. No GNIS "summit" or "ridge" feature named Crow's Nest exists in the Davis Creek/Reno Creek divide area of Big Horn or Rosebud County. The vantage is therefore officially unnamed in federal databases — itself a key constraint: any coordinate must come from scholarship, crowd-sourced mapping, or field GPS, not from GNIS.

### The physical/geographic envelope is well constrained
Multiple independent accounts place the vantage in the Wolf Mountains, on/just below the divide between Davis Creek (draining east to Rosebud Creek) and Reno/Ash/Sundance Creek (draining west to the Little Bighorn), east of the Little Bighorn valley, on high ground reaching ~4,400+ ft. Per Nathaniel Philbrick (*The Last Stand*), Varnum "awoke on the divide between the Rosebud and Little Bighorn rivers. He was lying in what he described as a 'peculiar hollow' nestled under a high [peak]" — i.e., a pocket below the crest where horses could be concealed, with scouts climbing higher to observe. This is consistent with the OSM candidate's elevation (4,439 ft) and position on the north-south Wolf Mountains ridge.

### Maguire map is accessible online
The official 1876 battlefield map — "Custer's Battle-Field (June 25th, 1876), Surveyed and drawn under the personal supervision of Lieut. Edward Maguire, Corps of Engineers, U.S.A., by Sergeant Charles Becker" — is digitized and freely viewable. Best direct high-resolution image: Northwestern University Libraries IIIF. Best full public-domain document: the Internet Archive scan of the *Annual Report of the Chief of Engineers* (1876), which carries Maguire's report (Appendix OO). At least two variants exist.

### The divide, the halt, and the vantage are three different points
The sources conflate these; the model must keep them separate:
- **The morning halt (Gray's "Halt #1"):** where the exhausted column stopped ~2:30–3:15 a.m. on June 25 and where troopers napped/ate breakfast, along Davis Creek, east of the vantage. This is NOT the vantage.
- **The divide crossing / Walter Camp brass marker (1917):** on the Rosebud–Little Bighorn divide, where Custer later made his division of command. The LBHA "Trail to Little Big Horn" page documents this brass marker placed by Walter M. Camp in 1917 "on the divide between the Rosebud and Little Bighorn valleys." This is where command was divided, NOT the observation vantage.
- **The vantage (Crow's Nest) itself:** the high pocket/peak south of the Davis Creek/Reno Creek divide from which the scouts observed the village at dawn. Candidate #1 corresponds to this.

### USGS quadrangle coverage
The Davis Creek / Reno Creek divide area is covered by the USGS 7.5-minute "Thompson Creek" and "Thompson Creek NW" quadrangles (Montana), per specialist forum discussion at the Little Bighorn History Alliance (a forum member wrote: "I have the USGS quadrangle topographical maps of the Thompson Creek and Thompson Creek NW areas. Custer's halt from 1003 to 1145 along Davis Creek…"). "Crows Nest" is not labeled as a named feature on these modern quads. Montana State Library provides downloadable DRG (Digital Raster Graphic) images of these quads (msl.mt.gov/geoinfo/data/DRG/24k).

### DELIVERABLE C — The Maguire 1876 Map

**Identity and creation.** Lt. Edward Maguire, engineer officer of the Department of Dakota (attached to Terry's column), reached the field on June 27, 1876. The survey was drawn under his supervision by Sergeant Charles Becker, Company D, Battalion of Engineers. Per author Robert Nightengale of Faribault, Minnesota (PR Newswire, 2016): "The original map was drawn on the battlefield two days after the battle by Chief Engineering Officer Lieutenant Edward Maguire, using a prismatic compass and an odometer cart, while the trails were fresh, survivors could be interviewed, and 7th Cavalry soldiers were being buried where they fell. This map must be considered accurate, but apparently remained with the Chief Engineer's Report of 1876 and was not produced at the Reno Court of Inquiry in 1879." Title: "Custer's Battle-Field (June 25th, 1876)." It accompanied Maguire's report in Appendix OO, *Report of the Secretary of War*, Vol. II, Part III, House Ex. Doc. 1, 44th Congress, 2nd Session (GPO, 1876). Per the LOC catalog: colored, ~39 × 45 cm, scale ca. 1:21,000, filed under "U.S. — Wars, Indian — Montana — 1876."

**Variants.** At least two exist: (1) the 1876 field-survey "first map" bound with the Chief of Engineers report; and (2) a later printed version referred to in the literature as "Version 8" / the "1881 Maguire map," reworked to reflect new testimony. Michael Donahue (*Drawing Battle Lines*, 2008) documents that Maguire produced eight subsequent versions; the version used at the 1879 Reno Court of Inquiry was, per Nightengale, "a poor reproduction of the original 1876 map." The Friends of the Little Bighorn "Investigating the Oxbows" archaeological report georeferenced all eight Maguire maps against 2005 orthophotography and USGS topos, reporting map-specific registration errors — for example, Maguire Map 3 showed "an RMS error of 120 meters or 393.7 feet or a 12% error (Scott and Donahue 2010:8)" and Map 5 "142.8 meters or 468.5 feet, or about 14% error." **These RMS figures are critical for the simulation: they quantify how much the 1876 cartography can be trusted when overlaid on modern terrain (expect ~120–145 m horizontal error).**

**What the map shows (for comparison against modern geography):** the 1876 course and bends of the Little Bighorn River and the fords/crossings; the timber in the valley where Reno's command fought; the extent and location of the Indian village along the west bank; Reno's skirmish line; the routes of the detachments under Custer, Reno, Benteen, and Keogh; and the marked graves of 12 fallen officers and men, including the three Custers (George, Tom, Boston), Keogh, Yates, Calhoun, Cooke, Smith, Reily, Crittenden, and Custer's nephew Autie Reed. Maguire's point "B" was his deduction of where Custer approached the village and was repulsed — a conclusion now disputed by scholars.

## Recommendations
1. **Primary candidate for viewshed testing:** Test candidate #1 (45.4454, -107.1392) first. Its geometry (≈21 km at a WNW bearing to Last Stand Hill; ≈24 km to the village site) and elevation (4,439 ft) match every documented constraint. If the earth-curvature-corrected viewshed yields line-of-sight to the Little Bighorn valley floor at ~14–15 miles while intervening ridges block the near-valley (consistent with seeing the far pony herd/smoke but a hazy, incomplete village view), promote this coordinate to the working vantage.
2. **Sensitivity sweep:** Because the OSM node's provenance is unverified, run the viewshed over a 1–2 km-radius search grid around #1, testing local high points on the same ridge, and score each against the constraint table in Deliverable B. Report the best-scoring grid cell separately; do not collapse it into #1.
3. **Exclude decoys explicitly:** Log candidates #2 and #3 only as decoy controls, and #4 as an unrelated GNIS feature. Never let any of these enter a blended coordinate.
4. **Maguire map georeferencing:** Use the Northwestern IIIF full-resolution image to georeference the 1876 map against a 2005+ orthophoto (the Friends of the Little Bighorn Oxbow study proves this is feasible), then compare the 1876 river course, ford locations, and village extent against modern channels for the Reno-fight terrain layer — carrying the study's ~120–145 m RMS registration error as an explicit uncertainty band.
5. **Thresholds that would change the recommendation:** If a specialist source (Gray's divide-crossing plate, a *Greasy Grass* or LBHA *Research Review* article, or an NPS study) supplies a coordinate more than ~1.5 km from #1, treat it as a co-equal HIGH-priority candidate and test it separately. A field GPS reading from the Mountain Pocket property would supersede all open-source candidates.

## Caveats
- **Reliability summary — strongest paper trail:** Ironically, the coordinates with the strongest paper trail (candidates #2, #3, #4) are all *wrong* for the simulation — they are documented decoys or unrelated features. The one coordinate that is geographically *right* (candidate #1) has the *weakest* provenance (crowd-sourced OpenStreetMap; its own contributor tagged it "Traditional Crows Nest?" with a question mark signaling uncertainty). This inversion is the central finding: the open literature does not contain a survey-grade published coordinate for the true vantage.
- **Sources that could not be accessed:** John S. Gray's *Custer's Last Campaign* divide-crossing map plates (not online); Michael Donahue's *Drawing Battle Lines* map plates; *Greasy Grass* (CBHMA annual) and LBHA *Research Review* articles that may contain field GPS readings; the LBHA proboards forum threads (JavaScript-gated, snippets only); and the specific University of Wyoming Digital UW Maguire object. Any of these could contain a more authoritative coordinate.
- **What a field visit or FOIA could add:** A GPS reading taken at the actual Mountain Pocket vantage would be definitive. An NPS Historic Resource Study or administrative history for Little Bighorn Battlefield NM (potentially FOIA-able) may map the June 1876 approach corridor and fix the vantage relative to the divide. The Crow Tribe/BIA may hold cadastral survey data for the private parcel.
- **Distance figures in popular sources vary** (13, 14, and 15 miles all appear, plus a "10 mi" figure for the regiment's own visible cook-fires); these are approximations from different authors and should be treated as a ±2-mile band, not precise measurements.
- **Coordinate arithmetic note:** The ~21 km distance and WNW bearing between candidate #1 and Last Stand Hill (≈45.570, -107.427) were computed by me from the two coordinates, not stated by any source; they are provided as a consistency check, not as a sourced measurement.
